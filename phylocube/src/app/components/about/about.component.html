Introduction

Conventionally, a Venn diagram is used to show concurrence of protein domain families between superkingdoms (Archaea, Bacteria, and Eukaryota) in order to research evolutionary relationships. However, Venn diagram has limitations; it is unable to capture the abundance of a protein domain families within a superkingdom. E.g., Venn diagram does not distinguish if a protein domain family is present only in a single species or in all species of kingdom. However, using a three-dimensional representation enables us to visualize and analyse the distribution of protein domain families in greater resolution (Figure 1)


What is a phyloCube?

Proteins are composed of one or more functional regions, known as domains. A lot of databases (e.g., Gene3D, SUPERFAMILY or Pfam) have assigned domains to complete proteomes using HMMs. Therefore, it is possible to gather information about the occurrence of a domain families in different genomes and ultimately in superkingdoms. By plotting the occurrence of protein domain families in three different axes (e.g. three superkingdoms) we will get a phyloCube. In the phyloCube, the location of each point (protein domain family) depends on the occurrence of that domain in different genomes in respective superkingdoms. For example, if a domain family is present in all archaea, bacteria, and eukaryotes it will be on the top-back-right corner (Figure 1). Primary, phyloCube is the visualisation of the global phylogenomic distribution of protein domain families.
